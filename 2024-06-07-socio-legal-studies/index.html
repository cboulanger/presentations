Socio-legal pluralism <img src="./uploads/038bc93e-3eb8-43f6-8882-a818b0206b6a.svg" class=left style="filter: drop-shadow(4px 4px 2px black); box-shadow:none;"/> <div class=right> - Many different terms being used, some tied to specific countries/regions (SLS; L&S), some having a different thematic focus (SLS vs. SoL) <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Sociological (in the broadest sense) approach to law with a distinct set of epistemological assumptions and questions <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Different from sociological jurisprudence or empirical legal studies, which use social science methods to answer jurisprudential questions <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> </div> Note: - Many different terms being used, some tied to specific countries/regions (SLS; L&S), some having a different thematic focus (SLS vs. SoL) - Used here as a shorthand for a **sociological (in the broadest sense) approach to law** with a distinct set of epistemological assumptions (law is a social practice not just a system of ideas) and questions (Erkenntnisinteressen): e.g., how does the law work? What is its function in society? - Different from sociological jurisprudence or empirical legal studies, which use social science methods to answer **jurisprudential questions** --- <!-- .slide: data-background="" --> ## Why should you care? - **Interlocutors & Audiences**: which people are sharing your questions and are interested in your answers? <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - **Theories**: Which theoretical discourses do you need to take into account, which ones can you safely ignore? <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - **Methods**: What methods are available that can generate data relevant for your questions? <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> --- <!-- .slide: data-background="" class="two-columns-header-footer" --> ## Theory, method, and data go together! <div class="left" style="transform: translateY(-10%);"> ```graphviz digraph { Question->Theory->Methods->Data->Analysis->Conclusions } ``` </div> <div class="right"> - Methods do not exist in isolation, they are embedded into assumptions that justify why the data they produce is meaningful <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - "Pure" social data does not exist, always collected after making methodological and/or theoretical choices <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Theories provide an epistemic and ontological frame (e.g., conceptual apparatus and basic assumptions) that make the results interoperable (and scientific progress can happen) <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> </div> --- <!-- .slide: data-background="" class="two-columns-header-footer" --> ## "Normal sciences" have paradigms - law, too? <div class="left two-images-with-captions"> <span class="image fragment" data-fragment-index="1"> ```graphviz digraph kuhn_cycle { rankdir=CIRC; // Arrange nodes in a circle pre_science [label="Pre-Science"]; normal_science [label="Normal Science"]; model_crisis [label="Model Crisis"]; paradigm_change [label="Paradigm Change"]; pre_science -> normal_science; normal_science -> model_crisis; model_crisis -> paradigm_change; paradigm_change -> normal_science; } ``` </span> <span class="image fragment" data-fragment-index="2"> ```graphviz digraph law_paradigms { rankdir=CIRC; // Arrange nodes in a circle hm [label="Majority opinion"]; mm [label="Minority opinion"]; lcd [label="Lower court decisions"]; acd [label="Apex court decision"]; hm -> mm -> lcd -> acd -> hm; } ``` </span> <span class="caption fragment" data-fragment-index="1">Kuhn Cycle</span> <span class="caption fragment" data-fragment-index="2">"Doctrinal Cycle"</span> </div> <div class="right"> - Kuhn's sociology of the natural sciences: succession of incommensurable paradigms following "crises" and "revolutions". Authority: fit of theory to empirical observations <!-- .element: class="fragment" data-fragment-index="1" --> - Authoritative organization of law: change in majority opinion and/or apex court decisions can change the doctrinal method/paradigm (e.g. Proportionality) <!-- .element: class="fragment" data-fragment-index="2"--> </div> Note: - Thomas Kuhn's sociology of the natural sciences: succession of incommensurable paradigms following "crises" and "revolutions" (i.e. Einstein's theory of relativity in physics). Authority: fit of theory to empirical observations - Authoritative organization of law: multiple (unempirical) "theories" exist simultaneously, but change in majority opinion and/or apex court decisions can change the doctrinal method/paradigm (e.g. proportionality) --- <!-- .slide: data-background="" class="two-columns-header-footer" --> ## The social sciences as "multiparadigmatic" disciplines <div class=left> <a href="https://cboulanger.github.io/jls-bibliometry/article-fig-01.png"> <img src=./uploads/73cdb525-abfe-4731-92f7-0d85db078572.png /> </a> <span class=caption>"Classic" authors in the Journal of Law and Society, 1974-2020</span> </div> <div class="right"> - Many different competing explanatory frameworks and epistemic perspectives <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Source of intellectual joy and methodological frustration! <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - No authority to decide which theory or method is the "right" one, different "lenses" on the empirical material <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> </div> --- <!-- .slide: data-background="" --> # 2. Perspectives in socio-legal studies Note: - What follows is obviously not a comprehensive overview over the available theoretical landscape, even not the major theoretical perspectives; very superficial - The aim is simply just to show the enormous differences in these perspectives, some of them incommensurable in the Kuhnian sense, existing side-by-side - We can discuss later whether that's an impediment to the progress of science or not --- <!-- .slide: data-background="" class="two-columns-header-footer" --> ## Marx: law as the tool of the economically powerful <img src="./uploads/1813e12f-2c76-47c5-8ed6-44dda630dccb.png" width="303vw" class="left" /> <div class="right"> - Marx (1818-1883): provides an anti-legal theory: law is the expression of underlying modes of production, a dependent variable <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - "law-as-mere-superstructure" version is unconvincing. <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Remains critically useful: null-hypothesis, materialism/anti-idealism, inseparability of law, power and the economy <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> </div> Note: - Marx provides an anti-legal theory: law is the expression of underlying modes of production, a dependent variable - The "dumbed-down" version based on the notion that law is mere superstructure (law nothing more than a smokescreen, mere instrument of ruling classes, "false consciousness", has no autonomy at all) is unconvincing. - However, a Marxist perspective remains very fruitful: as null-Hypothesis, materialism/anti-idealism, focus on interest groups, power relationships, inseparability of law and economic phenomena - Inspired many socio-legal thinkers, dominant in 1970s/1980s, then decline --- <!-- .slide: data-background="" class="two-columns-header-footer" --> ## Durkheim: law as an experession of social solidarity <img src="./uploads/9d86bd1f-b50b-4849-ae16-13000cb7e8ab.png" width="303vw" class="left" /> <div class="right"> - Durkheim (1858-1917): Theorist of social differentiation and social integration through law. Emphasized the social function of law <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Focused on criminal law as the expression of social values, neglecting the role of actors and interest groups <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Provided us with an analytic perspective on the role of law in social relations, "functional" view of law <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> </div> Note: - Durkheim (1858-1917): Theorist of social differentiation and social integration through law. Emphasized the social function of law - Focused on criminal law as the expression of social values, neglecting the role of actors and interest groups - Provided us with an analytic perspective on the role in social relations - Influenced functionalist analysis of law (Parsons), which was later transformed by Systems Theory --- <!-- .slide: data-background="" class="two-columns-header-footer" --> ## Weber: law as rational authority <img src=./uploads/cf958ee3-c934-43c2-8707-c28ebb11fc02.png class="left"> <div class=right> - Max Weber (1864-1920): focus on legal thinking, legal-bureaucratic apparatus, rational-legal legitimacy <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Sociology of Law as part of a sociology of the state and the economy, centered on the establishment of order, very "German" <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Still very influential, interfaces easily with lawyer's interests, middle-range theory based on ideal types <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> </div> Note: - Max Weber focus on legal thinking, legal-bureaucratic apparatus, rational-legal legitimacy - Sociology of Law as part of a sociology of the state and the economy, very "German" - Despite showing its age, Weberian paradigm still very influential, interfaces easily with lawyer's interests - Fragmented work, not an overarching social theory, so no "school", but a constant methodological presence, mainly in historical sociology of law --- <!-- .slide: data-background="" class="two-columns-header-footer" --> ## Systems Theory: law as an autonomous system <a href="./uploads/a94c68d4-97a1-4781-9f88-fc77db22c14e.png"> <img src=./uploads/a94c68d4-97a1-4781-9f88-fc77db22c14e.png class=left /> </a> <div class=right> - Niklas Luhmann (1927-1998) / Gunther Teubner (1944): Law as an autopoietic (self-referential, closed) subsystem of society <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Law is separated from other functional subsystems (economy, politics, religion etc.) <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Completely new vocabulary; law as communication about "legal" vs "illegal"; interested more in semantic orders than in power, inequality, actors, institutions <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> </div> Note: - Law as an autopoietic (self-referential, closed to its environment) subsystem of society separated from other functional subsystems (economy, politics, religion etc.) - Completely new vocabulary; law as communication about "legal" vs "illegal"; interested more in semantic orders than in power, inequality, actors, institutions - Most developed sociological account of the autonomy of law generated by a historical process of social differentiation - Because of conciseness of theory and conceptual apparatus, strong "school" developed - Highly compatible with German legal doctrine both in its intellectual ambition and ontology (law as a system) ---- <img src=./uploads/a94c68d4-97a1-4781-9f88-fc77db22c14e.png /> --- ## "French Theory": conceptual contributions to socio-legal theory <!-- .slide: data-background="" class="two-columns-header-footer" --> <img src=./uploads/7c703da9-8634-41c4-875f-09880f18154d.png class=left /> <div class="right"> - Foucault: Law as a "dispositif": apparatus of elements including discourses, institutions, even architecture, which organize power/knowledge and controls society <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Bourdieu: Law as a "field" in which actors with different forms of juridic "capital" struggle over the monopoly of defining what the law says. Lawyers as "gardiens de l'hypocrisie collective" <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> </div> Note: - Foucault: Law as a "dispositif": apparatus of elements including discourses, institutions, even architecture, which organize power/knowledge and controls society - Bourdieu: Law as a "field" in which actors with different forms of juridic "capital" struggle over the monopoly of defining what the law says. Lawyers as "gardiens de l'hypocrisie collective" --- <!-- .slide: data-background="" class="two-columns-header-footer" --> ## Empirical analyses as a part/form of social criticism <img src=./uploads/040dcf10-e725-4650-af69-91786b109231.png class=left /> <div class="right"> - Critical legal studies as part of (post-)Marxist social criticism <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Feminist legal theory as part of the Feminist movement <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Law and gender, queerness, race (critical whiteness), intersectionality <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> </div> Note: - Feminist legal theory; Contentious relationship with legal gender/queer studies, legal critical whiteness studies - Focus is on the role of law in constructing social relationships, discriminatory practices etc. --- <!-- .slide: data-background="" class="two-columns-header-footer" --> ## Empirical but not socio-legal? <br/> Empirical legal studies/ Law & Economy <img src=./uploads/72a57a91-4dc8-4429-9d25-0cdf752d98ee.png class=left /> <div class="right"> - Solving legal questions with social science, mostly statistical methods and economic theory, mostly affirmative <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Successful "paradigm": pluralistic community sharing conceptual and methodological apparatus <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Self-sufficient, very little interaction with socio-legal community <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Critical version: Law & Political Economy: focusing on law's role in creating economic inequality <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> </div> Note: - Solving legal questions with social science, mostly statistical methods and economic theory - Little interest in questions that cannot be solved by these methods or alternative explanations, often unreflected application of economic theory on non-economic domains. - "Individualistic Materialism" shares with Marxism function of "Null-hypothesis": Assuming self-interest, what can empirical evidence say about "real" motivation. Newer developments: "Analytic narratives", Behavioural economics - Because of theoretical coherence (and organizational marketing), very successful research programme --- # 3. Normativity and socio-legal histories Note: - We have seen that the various theoretical perspectives exist in the socio-legal tradition, but it is still unclear what "socio-legal" means for research that is done today - There is no one single approach, no only due to the multiparadigmaticity of the social sciences, but also because the development of socio-legal studies is very different in different parts of the world - a) context - very different relationship to the main disciplines (sociology & law), b) different attitudes towards normativity --- <!-- .slide: class="two-columns-header-footer" --> ## Question of normativity: it's simple (?) <img src="./uploads/b5ec1706-3bbd-4160-b364-ef14aafe2d82.jpg" class="left"> <div class="right"> > "[I]t must be clear that a sociology of law is essentially different from a legal science (Rechtswissenschaft) in terms of subject matter and method, the task of which is to recognize not **what actually is, but what ought to be** based on the law; a form of **evaluative, not explanatory**, in other words: normative legal science." Hans Kelsen (1915), reviewing Eugen Ehrlich's "A Foundation of a Sociology of Law" (1913) </div> --- ## Question of normativity: <br> it's more complicated than that --- <img src=./uploads/00ab0b57-2fc9-4d12-baf3-a138bfd52ab1.svg style="box-shadow: none"/> Note: -
