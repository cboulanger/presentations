group of disciplines that applies a social scientific perspective to the study of law, including the sociology of law, legal anthropology, legal history, psychology and the law, political science studies of courts, and science-oriented comparativists. > > These various approaches to law are joined by more than just their scientific orientation. Broadly speaking, **the glue which bonds this diverse group is a left to-far-left critical orientation to law. Most socio-legal scholars are ‘progressives’**." (Tamanaha 1998) --- > "For many, the label 'socio-legal studies' conjures up a sense of **vaguely leftist, rather outmoded perspectives, which masks values under spurious claims to objectivity**" (Jabbari 1998, reviwing Tamanaha) --- > "[A]s sociolegal scholars we need to analyse the impact of our decisions on others and to take a stand against unfair and unjust distribution of resources whether at the local, national or international level. **We need a vision of a just society which is informed by moral indignation**." (Hilliard 2002) --- > "With many other legal academics, what this writer initially sought from sociolegal work was a set of new perspectives on law to allow a **breakout from the claustrophobic world of legal scholarship and education**, as previously encountered." (Cotterrell 2002) --- > "The studies of law in its social context are therefore characterised by a **myriad of ‘sectarian’ alternative approaches**, each motivated by its own specific scientific goal and political interest. This diversity of perspectives on law and legal order is a **source of inspiration and confusion**." (Banakar 1998) --- <!-- .slide: class="column-layout" --> ### Example: Galanter, Why the ‘Haves’ Come out Ahead (1974) <div> ![](./uploads/ae91afa1-53b4-4671-9cfa-3931d32c7bb9.png) </div> <div style="flex:3" class="font-medium"> - Galanter, Marc. 1974. “Why the ‘Haves’ Come out Ahead. Authority: fit of theory to empirical observations <!-- .element: class="fragment" data-fragment-index="1" --> - Authoritative organization of law: change in majority opinion and/or apex court decisions can change the doctrinal method/paradigm (e.g. Proportionality) <!-- .element: class="fragment" data-fragment-index="2"--> - What is the authority for truth claims of socio-legal studies? <!-- .element: class="fragment" data-fragment-index="3"--> </div> --- ## Paradigmatic Theory vs. middle-range theory > “Theories of the middle range [are] theories that lie between the minor but necessary working hypotheses that evolve in abundance during day-to-day research and the all-inclusive systematic efforts to develop a unified theory that will explain all the observed uniformities of social behavior, social organization and social change. (Merton 1957: 39) --- <!-- .slide: class="column-layout" --> ## The social sciences as "multiparadigmatic" disciplines <div> <div class="shrink-on-click fragment"> <img src=./uploads/73cdb525-abfe-4731-92f7-0d85db078572.png ></img> </div> <span class="font-medium">"Classic" authors in the Journal of Law and Society, 1974-2020</span> <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> </div> <div> - Many different competing explanatory frameworks and epistemic perspectives <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Source of intellectual joy and methodological frustration! <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - No authority to decide which theory or method is the "right" one, different "lenses" on the empirical material <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> </div> --- ## 2. --- <!-- .slide: class="column-layout" --> ### Marx: law as the tool of the economically powerful <div> <img src="./uploads/1813e12f-2c76-47c5-8ed6-44dda630dccb.png" width="303vw" /> </div> <div style="flex:2"> - Marx (1818-1883): provides an anti-legal theory: law is the expression of underlying modes of production, a dependent variable <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - "law-as-mere-superstructure" version is unconvincing. <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Remains critically useful: "null-hypothesis" for empirical theories of law, materialism/anti-idealism, inseparability of law, power and the economy (-> LPE) <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> </div> Note: - Marx is not a sociologist of law, but provided a valuable "anti-legal" theory: law is the expression of underlying modes of production, a dependent variable - The "dumbed-down" version based on the notion that law is mere superstructure (law nothing more than a smokescreen, mere instrument of ruling classes, "false consciousness", has no autonomy at all) is unconvincing. - However, a Marxist perspective remains very fruitful: as null-Hypothesis, materialism/anti-idealism, focus on interest groups, power relationships, inseparability of law and economic phenomena - Inspired many socio-legal thinkers, dominant in 1970s/1980s, then decline --- <!-- .slide: class="column-layout" --> ### Durkheim: law as an expression of social solidarity <div> <img src="./uploads/9d86bd1f-b50b-4849-ae16-13000cb7e8ab.png" width="303vw" /> </div> <div style="flex:2"> - Durkheim (1858-1917): Theorist of social differentiation and social integration through law. Emphasized the social function of law <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Focused on criminal law as the expression of social values rather than on the role of actors and interest groups <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Provided us with an analytic perspective on the role of law in social relations, "functional" view of law <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> </div> --- <!-- .slide: class="column-layout" --> ### Weber: law as rational authority <div> <img src=./uploads/cf958ee3-c934-43c2-8707-c28ebb11fc02.png > </div> <div style="flex:2"> - Max Weber (1864-1920): focus on legal thinking, legal-bureaucratic apparatus, rational-legal legitimacy <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Sociology of Law as part of a sociology of the state and the economy, centered on the establishment of order. Fragmented work, not an overarching social theory, very historical <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Still very influential & productive, interfaces easily with lawyer's interests, middle-range theory based on ideal types, law as a set of rational rules + enforcement staff <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> </div> --- <!-- .slide: class="column-layout" --> ### Eugen Ehrlich: Legal Pluralism avant la lettre <div> ![](./uploads/3fb4b54d-e4ec-4bc2-bfee-445025a7bc9f.png) </div> <div style="flex:2"> - Eugen Ehrlich (1862-1922): Wrote the "Fundamental Principles of the Sociology of Law", often referred to as its "founder" <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - In contrast to Weber, emphasizes on law from below: "Living Law" as opposed to official law, as it could be observed in the peasant population in the Bukovina <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - General idea hugely influential, but more in terms of inspiration than close reading. Precursor to "legal pluralism" <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> </div> --- <!-- .slide: class="column-layout" --> ### Systems Theory: law as an autonomous system <div> ![](./uploads/0c5bc6ed-94b0-4a60-9a96-4e1dac6e7785.png) </div> <div style="flex:2"> - Niklas Luhmann (1927-1998) / Gunther Teubner (1944): Law as an autopoietic (self-referential, closed) subsystem of society <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Law is separated from other functional subsystems (economy, politics, religion etc.) <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Completely new vocabulary; law as communication, binary code "legal" vs "illegal"; interested more in semantic structure than in power, inequality, actors, institutions - but "critical" ST exists <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> </div> --- <!-- .slide: class="column-layout" --> ### "French Theory" <div> ![](./uploads/7c703da9-8634-41c4-875f-09880f18154d.png) <!-- .element: class="fragment" data-fragment-index="1" --> ![](./uploads/dfef50bc-afb1-4a9d-8316-25f4e6a70455.png) <!-- .element: class="fragment" data-fragment-index="2" --> </div> <div style="flex:2"> - **Foucault**: Law as a "dispositif": apparatus of elements including discourses, institutions, even architecture, which organize power/knowledge in society <!-- .element: class="fragment" data-fragment-index="1" --> - **Bourdieu**: Theory of practice, law as a "field" in which actors with different forms of juridical "capital" struggle over the monopoly of defining what the law says. Lawyers as "guardians of collective hypocricy", legal "habitus" <!-- .element: class="fragment" data-fragment-index="2" --> </div> --- <!-- .slide: class="column-layout" --> ### Empirical analyses as a part/form of social criticism <div> ![](./uploads/040dcf10-e725-4650-af69-91786b109231.png) </div> <div style="flex:2"> - Critical legal studies as part of (post-)Marxist social criticism <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Feminist legal theory as part of the feminist movement, critique of patriachical nature of law <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Law and gender, queerness, race (critical whiteness), intersectionality <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> </div> --- <!-- .slide: class="column-layout" --> ### Empirical, but socio-legal?<br/>Empirical legal studies and Law & Economy <div> ![](./uploads/72a57a91-4dc8-4429-9d25-0cdf752d98ee.png) </div> <div style="flex:3" class="font-medium"> - Solving legal questions with social science, mostly statistical methods and economic theory. - "Individualistic Materialism" assuming actor's self-interest. Newer developments: "Analytic narratives", Behavioural economics <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Successful "paradigm": well-organized community sharing a conceptual and methodological apparatus -> successful research programme <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> - Critique: often unreflected application of economic theory on non-economic domains. Critical version: Law & Political Economy: focusing on law's role in creating economic inequality <!-- .element: class="fragment" --> </div> --- ## 3.
